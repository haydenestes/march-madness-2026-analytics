# Pool Strategy — 2026 March Madness

**Last Updated:** March 16, 2026
**Framework:** Game Theory + Ownership Modeling + KenPom Efficiency

---

## The Core Principle

**You are not trying to predict every game correctly. You are trying to build a bracket that outperforms other entries in your specific pool.**

This distinction is everything. The "most accurate" bracket is often not the "best pool bracket." Picking the most likely winner in every game produces a bracket that looks identical to 60% of other entries — and a 60th-percentile finish.

The goal is **expected pool equity**, not accuracy.

### The Duke Problem

If Duke wins the national championship:
- ~38% of pools have Duke as champion
- You need Arizona or someone else to accumulate enough points to offset
- **Winning when 38% of opponents also win = minimal equity gain**

If Duke loses early (our pick — Kansas in Sweet 16):
- ~65% of pools lose their Final Four pick early
- Your bracket stays alive while over half the competition is eliminated
- **This is the highest-equity scenario in the field**

**Rule: Never pick the most popular champion in a large pool.**

In a 10-person pool, maybe you pick Duke (low field). In a 100+ person pool, fade Duke aggressively.

Auburn's path looks similar but they'd need to beat Michigan, who they share a region with. In the realistic scenario, Michigan has the easier expected draw.

## Pool Size Adjustments

### Small Pool (10-20 people)
- Be less contrarian: Arizona or Florida as champion is fine
- Skip one level of upsets — you don't need as much differentiation
- Focus on Roster Madness picks where ownership variance is highest

### Medium Pool (20-100 people)
- This analysis is calibrated for this pool size
- Arizona champion, Michigan Final Four, Houston upset Florida
- Full upset slate: Iowa, Texas A&M, VCU, BYU

### Large Pool (100+ people)
- Go more contrarian: Consider Auburn or Houston as champion
- Double down on VCU, BYU, Texas A&M upsets
- Michigan as champion (8% ownership) becomes viable

## Roster Madness Specific Strategy

Roster Madness scores by projected seed performance across 16 picks (one per seed slot). This is a different game than bracket picking.

**Key differences:**
1. You pick a team per seed slot — so your "8-seed" must win their opening game to score
2. Upsets hurt you in the slot of the losing team, not the winning team
3. **You want teams that win at least one game at every seed level**

### High-Leverage Roster Madness Picks (9/10/11 tier)
The 9/10/11 seeds score big if they win one game. Most people autopick chalk here:

## The Three Rules That Win Pools

1. **Never pick the consensus favorite as champion in a large pool.** The math doesn't work.
2. **Always find the best KenPom mismatches in 8/9, 7/10, 6/11 games.** These are worth 2x-4x the points per risk unit.
3. **Pick Roster Madness teams, not bracket picks, for value slots 9-11.** Different games require different strategies.
